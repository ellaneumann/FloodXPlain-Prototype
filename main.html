⊞ Combined Sewer System (CSO)

COMBINED A single pipe carries both stormwater and raw sewage. During heavy rain the system overflows — raw sewage discharges directly into creeks. This is a Combined Sewer Overflow (CSO).

Atlanta's combined sewer core covers roughly 13 square miles (about 10% of the collection system) spanning downtown, Midtown, Georgia Tech, and east Atlanta near Grant Park.

In 1995 the EPA and Upper Chattahoochee Riverkeeper sued Atlanta for Clean Water Act violations. The 1998 Federal Consent Decree committed the city to $3+ billion in fixes. The program runs through 2027.

→ Clean Water Atlanta: Full consent decree history

⊟ Sanitary Sewer System (SSO)

SANITARY A separate pipe carries only household sewage to treatment plants. Stormwater drains through a completely different system (MS4 — Municipal Separate Storm Sewer).

About 90% of Atlanta's collection system uses separate sanitary piping. These still overflow (SSOs) when pipes collapse, block, or become infiltrated by groundwater. A 1999 Second Consent Decree addressed SSOs with a 2014 deadline.

→ Clean Water Atlanta: Consent Decree Program

⚠️ Key Difference

When a combined sewer overflows, raw sewage contaminates streets, yards, and creeks — geysers erupt from manholes. When a sanitary sewer overflows, the same happens but the storm drain still works. Both violate the Clean Water Act.

Proctor Creek and Intrenchment Creek are most affected by CSOs. The 2002 Vine City flood and 2012 Peoplestown disaster both involved combined sewer overflows mixing sewage with stormwater.

Flood Risk Score Formula
Score (0–100) =
 FEMA zone overlap    × 35%
+ Impervious surface %  × 25%
+ Historic flood events  × 25%
+ Low elevation score   × 15%

± Pipe material modifier
  VCP / CMP pipes: +5 to +10 pts

FEMA Zone Overlap (35%)

Percent of watershed intersecting FEMA Zone AE (1% annual chance flood) from the National Flood Hazard Layer. → FEMA Map Service Center

Impervious Surface (25%)

Percent impervious cover from USGS NLCD 2021. High imperviousness means faster runoff that overwhelms stormwater capacity. → USGS NLCD Data

Historic Flood Events (25%)

Normalized count of NOAA Storm Events flood reports within each watershed, 2000–2025. → NOAA Storm Events DB

Elevation / Topography (15%)

Low-lying areas relative to watershed average, from USGS 3DEP elevation data. → USGS 3DEP

Pipe Material Modifier

VCP (vitrified clay pipe) and CMP (corrugated metal pipe) add +5 to +10 pts due to higher failure probability. Sourced from COA stormpipe.shp. → COA GIS Open Data
